# Data Representation and Preprocessing
## DHS Repatriation Cleaning
The DHS repatriation dataset was cleaned and transformed into a consistent monthly time series so it could be aligned with the other datasets. Column names were standardized to remove formatting issues, and key variables were renamed for clarity. The fiscal month and year fields were converted into a proper calendar-based date by adjusting the fiscal month index and shifting the year when needed. This ensured that months like October to December were correctly mapped to the previous calendar year.
After cleaning, the dataset contains 182 monthly observations spanning from October 2009 to November 2024. There were no missing values or duplicate dates, confirming that the dataset is structured cleanly at one observation per month. We also verified that there are no gaps in the timeline, meaning the time series has complete monthly coverage. Summary statistics show that repatriations average around 61,841, with values ranging from 27,080 to 147,080, indicating substantial variation over time.
## CBP Encounters Cleaning and Aggregation
The CBP encounter data were provided across two files with overlapping fiscal years, so the first step was removing fiscal year 2023 from the older dataset to avoid duplication. After combining both files, the dataset contained 83,920 rows spanning fiscal years 2020 through 2026.
Column names were then standardized, and month abbreviations were converted into numeric values. Since the data were recorded using fiscal years, we converted them into calendar dates by shifting October through December into the previous year. This created a consistent monthly date field that aligns with the DHS dataset.
Before aggregation, there were a large number of duplicate dates, which is expected because the raw data contains multiple rows per month across different categories. After grouping by date, the dataset was reduced to 76 unique monthly observations with no duplicate dates. A separate aggregation was also created for Title 42 encounters, resulting in 39 monthly observations corresponding to when that policy was active.
There were no missing values in key fields, no missing months, and the final date range spans from October 2019 to January 2026. 
## Policy Dataset Cleaning
The policy dataset was converted into a standardized monthly time series to match the DHS and CBP datasets. The raw month column was parsed into a proper datetime format, and column names were cleaned for consistency, including renaming the administration variable for clarity.
The cleaned dataset contains 132 monthly observations from January 2015 through December 2025. There were no missing values, no invalid dates, and no duplicate entries, confirming that the dataset is clean and structured correctly. We also verified that there are no gaps in the timeline, meaning the dataset has full monthly coverage.
## Merging Datasets
All datasets were merged using the standardized monthly date field to create a single analytical dataset. An inner join was used between the DHS and CBP datasets to ensure only overlapping months were included, while left joins were used for the Title 42 and policy datasets to preserve all available observations.
The final merged dataset contains 62 monthly observations and 15 variables, covering the period from October 2019 to November 2024. There were no missing values or duplicate dates, confirming that the merge was successful and maintained a consistent one row per month structure. Missing values in the Title 42 encounter variable were replaced with zero to represent months with no activity rather than missing data. We also verified that there are no gaps in the timeline, meaning the dataset has continuous monthly coverage. 
## Feature Engineering
Several features were created to capture enforcement dynamics and policy effects over time. The primary feature is the repatriation to encounter ratio, which measures the relationship between removals and encounters each month and serves as a key indicator of enforcement intensity. A Title 42 share variable was also created to measure the proportion of encounters processed under Title 42.
To capture temporal patterns, a one-month lag of the ratio and a three-month rolling average were added. These features help model short term dependencies and smooth fluctuations in the data.
The engineered features were generated successfully with no missing values in the main ratio variables. Missing values only appear in the lag (1 value) and rolling average (2 values), which are expected due to edge effects at the beginning of the time series. There were no months with zero encounters and no infinite values, confirming that the calculations are stable.
Summary statistics show that the repatriation to encounter ratio has a mean of approximately 0.57, with values ranging from 0.18 to 1.24. Most values fall between 0.34 and 0.80, indicating a relatively stable baseline. A few months have values above 1, which likely reflects timing differences between encounters and repatriations as well as policy driven changes such as the implementation of Title 42. Title 42 share ranges from 0 to 0.73, showing how reliance on the policy evolved over time. Overall, these features capture both baseline behavior and key structural changes, providing a strong foundation for further analysis and modeling.
## Final Table
The final dataset represents a fully integrated monthly time series that combines DHS repatriations, CBP encounters, Title 42 activity, and policy indicators into a single structured table. After merging and feature engineering, the dataset contains 62 observations and 15+ variables, with each row representing one month from October 2019 through November 2024.
The dataset includes core variables such as repatriations and encounters, along with derived features like the repatriation to encounter ratio, Title 42 share, lagged values, and rolling averages. Policy indicators and administration labels were also retained to support analysis of how enforcement patterns change under different policy environments.
We verified that the dataset has no missing values, no duplicate dates, and no gaps in the monthly timeline, ensuring that it is complete and consistent. All variables are aligned at the same level of granularity (monthly), which is critical for time series analysis and modeling.
Overall, this final table serves as a clean, analysis ready dataset that integrates multiple sources into a unified structure. It captures both operational metrics and policy context, providing a strong foundation for downstream modeling, trend analysis, and evaluation of enforcement dynamics over time.
